Q1.Discuss in detail the four generations of computers, mentioning the core technology and key limitation each generation solved.
10 Marks▼ show answer

First Generation (1940s–1950s)

Built using vacuum tubes as the core switching component. Machines such as ENIAC were enormous, generated huge amounts of heat, consumed massive electricity, and were highly unreliable since tubes burned out frequently. Programming was done in machine language, making operation extremely slow and specialised.

Second Generation (1950s–1960s)

Vacuum tubes were replaced by transistors, which were smaller, more durable, generated far less heat, and consumed less power. This solved the size and reliability problems of the first generation. Assembly language emerged, making programming somewhat more manageable.

Third Generation (1960s–1970s)

The introduction of the Integrated Circuit (IC) allowed thousands of transistors to be placed on a single chip. Computers became smaller, faster, and more affordable, solving the bulkiness and cost issues of earlier machines. This generation also introduced high-level programming languages (like FORTRAN and COBOL), making software development accessible to a wider range of people.

Fourth Generation (1970s–present)

The invention of the microprocessor packed an entire CPU onto one chip. This solved the remaining size and cost barriers, giving birth to the personal computer and enabling computers to enter homes, schools, and small offices for the first time.

Exam TipAlways state generation → core technology → the specific limitation it fixed from the previous generation. This structure earns full marks consistently.
Q2.Differentiate between hardware and software, and further explain the difference between system software and application software with examples.
10 Marks▼ show answer

Hardware vs Software

Hardware consists of the physical, tangible components of a computer system — the CPU, RAM, monitor, keyboard, mouse, and printer. Hardware can be seen, touched, and can physically wear out or break over time.

Software is the intangible collection of instructions (programs) that direct hardware to perform tasks. It cannot be touched, only stored and executed; rather than wearing out, software develops logical errors known as "bugs." Hardware and software are interdependent — hardware is useless without software's instructions, and software cannot run without hardware to execute it on.

System Software vs Application Software

System software manages and controls the computer's own internal resources so that other programs can run smoothly. It works mostly in the background. Examples: Operating Systems (Windows, Linux), device drivers, and utility programs (antivirus, disk cleanup tools).

Application software is designed for the end-user to accomplish specific real-world tasks. Examples: MS Word for typing documents, Excel for spreadsheets, and web browsers for accessing the internet.

Q3.Discuss in detail the types of computers based on size and processing capacity, comparing their power, number of users, and typical applications.
10 Marks▼ show answer

Supercomputer

The fastest and most powerful category, capable of massively parallel processing — performing trillions of operations per second. Used for weather forecasting, nuclear research, climate modelling, and large-scale scientific simulations that no smaller machine could handle in reasonable time.

Mainframe Computer

A large, highly reliable system built to serve hundreds or thousands of users simultaneously. Commonly used by banks, airlines, and government departments for high-volume, mission-critical transaction processing where uptime and data integrity are essential.

Minicomputer

Smaller and less powerful than a mainframe, but still able to support multiple simultaneous users. Typically used by mid-sized businesses or individual departments within a larger organisation for tasks that don't require mainframe-level capacity.

Microcomputer

Built around a single microprocessor chip, designed for one user at a time — the personal computers, laptops, and workstations found in homes and offices. Affordable and versatile, but far less powerful than the categories above.

Q4.What is a Computer Based Information System (CBIS)? Discuss each of its five components in detail.
10 Marks▼ show answer

A Computer Based Information System (CBIS) uses computer technology to perform some or all of an organisation's information-processing tasks, converting raw data into accurate, timely, and useful information for decision-making. It is built from five interdependent components.

1. Hardware

The physical devices — processors, storage units, input/output equipment — that physically carry out data processing.

2. Software

The programs (system and application software) that instruct the hardware on exactly how to process data.

3. Data

The raw, unprocessed facts and figures the system collects and feeds in, which become information once processed.

4. People

The end-users who rely on the system's output, and the IT staff (programmers, analysts, administrators) who build and maintain it.

5. Procedures

The documented rules, policies, and steps that govern how the system should correctly be used and maintained, ensuring consistency and security.

All five components must work together; removing any one of them — for instance, having hardware and software but no trained people or clear procedures — causes the system to fail in practice even if it works in theory.

Q5.Discuss in detail the various methods of input and processing data used by organisations, with appropriate examples.
10 Marks▼ show answer

Methods of Input

Keyboard entry: Manual typing of data; flexible and universal but slower and more prone to human error.

Source Data Automation (SDA): Data is captured directly and electronically at its point of origin using devices such as barcode scanners, OCR readers, or MICR — drastically reducing transcription errors and speeding up entry. Example: scanning a product barcode at a supermarket till.

Methods of Processing

Batch Processing: Transactions are collected over a period of time and processed together in one scheduled run. This is efficient for high-volume, non-urgent work. Example: processing an entire organisation's monthly payroll in one overnight run.

Online / Real-time Processing: Each transaction is processed immediately as it occurs, updating records instantly. This is essential where delay is unacceptable. Example: an ATM withdrawal, where the account balance must update the moment cash is dispensed, or an airline seat booking system where two agents must never be allowed to sell the same seat.

Organisations often combine these methods — using SDA or keyboard entry to capture data, and choosing batch or real-time processing depending on how urgently that data needs to be acted upon.

Q6.Explain in detail the three models of organising a computing facility — Centralized, Distributed, and Decentralized — along with their advantages and disadvantages.
10 Marks▼ show answer

Centralized Computing Facility

All processing power and data reside on one large central computer, accessed by users through terminals.

  • Advantages: Easy to secure, control, and maintain consistent data from one location.
  • Disadvantages: Single point of failure; can bottleneck under heavy demand; expensive to scale.

Distributed Computing Facility

Processing is spread across multiple connected computers — often in different locations — that cooperate and share data as one coordinated system.

  • Advantages: Resilient (failure of one node doesn't stop the whole system); scales well; brings processing closer to where data is generated.
  • Disadvantages: Harder to coordinate, synchronise, and secure across many connected nodes.

Decentralized Computing Facility

Each department or location runs its own independent computer system with little or no central coordination.

  • Advantages: Maximum local control and fast local decision-making, tailored to each unit's needs.
  • Disadvantages: Duplicated effort, inconsistent data across departments, and difficulty consolidating organisation-wide information.

In practice, most modern organisations use a hybrid approach — central oversight of critical systems combined with distributed or decentralized elements where local flexibility matters more.

Q7.Discuss the importance and limitations of computers in modern life, giving examples from different fields.
10 Marks▼ show answer

Importance

  • Speed: Performs millions of operations per second — e.g. processing thousands of bank transactions instantly.
  • Accuracy: Produces near-zero errors when correctly programmed, vital in fields like medical diagnostics and engineering calculations.
  • Storage: Holds vast amounts of data compactly — entire libraries' worth of records on a single server.
  • Tirelessness: Performs repetitive tasks (like payroll runs) without fatigue or boredom-driven mistakes.

These strengths have transformed education (e-learning platforms), business (automated record-keeping), healthcare (digital patient records, diagnostic imaging), and communication (instant messaging, video conferencing) — making processes faster, cheaper, and far more reliable than manual alternatives.

Limitations

  • No judgement: Computers cannot use common sense or intuition; they only do what they are instructed — captured by the principle "Garbage In, Garbage Out."
  • Dependency: Require continuous electricity and correctly written programs to function at all.
  • Cost: High-performance systems can be expensive to acquire, maintain, and upgrade.
  • Security risks: Vulnerable to viruses, hacking, and data breaches without proper safeguards.

In short, computers dramatically extend human capability but remain entirely dependent on correct human instruction and proper maintenance to be useful.
