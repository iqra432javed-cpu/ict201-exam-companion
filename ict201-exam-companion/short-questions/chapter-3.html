16 most important short questions on Networking & The Internet.

Q1.Differentiate between Twisted Pair, Coaxial, and Fiber Optic cables.
5 Marks▼ show answer
  • Twisted Pair: Two twisted copper wires; cheap, used in LANs and telephone lines, limited range/speed.
  • Coaxial: A shielded copper conductor; better interference resistance, historically used for cable TV.
  • Fiber Optic: Transmits light through glass/plastic strands; the fastest and most interference-immune medium, used for internet backbones.
Q2.What is a protocol? Give two examples.
5 Marks▼ show answer

A protocol is a set of agreed rules that govern how data is formatted, transmitted, and received between devices, ensuring reliable communication between different systems.

Examples: TCP/IP (core internet addressing/delivery) and HTTP (governs web page requests and delivery).

Q3.Differentiate between Star and Bus network topologies.
5 Marks▼ show answer

In a Star topology, all devices connect to one central hub/switch — easy to manage, but the whole network fails if the hub fails.

In a Bus topology, all devices share a single common backbone cable — cheap and simple, but a break anywhere in that cable disrupts the entire network.

Q4.What is Ring topology? State one advantage and one disadvantage.
5 Marks▼ show answer

In a Ring topology, each device connects to exactly two neighbouring devices, forming a closed loop through which data travels.

Advantage: Data travels in a predictable, organised path. Disadvantage: A single broken connection can disrupt the whole ring, unless a dual-ring design is used.

Q5.Differentiate between LAN and WAN.
5 Marks▼ show answer

A LAN (Local Area Network) covers a small area such as one building or campus, offering high speed at lower cost.

A WAN (Wide Area Network) covers a much larger geographic area — cities, countries, or globally — connecting multiple LANs together, but is typically slower and more expensive due to distance. The Internet itself is the largest example of a WAN.

Q6.What is ARPANET? Where was its first node established?
5 Marks▼ show answer

ARPANET was a project funded by the U.S. Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) in the late 1960s, designed to let research computers communicate and build a resilient communication network — it became the technical ancestor of today's Internet.

Its first node, often called its "birthplace," was established at UCLA (University of California, Los Angeles) in 1969.

Q7.What role did TCP/IP play in the development of the Internet?
5 Marks▼ show answer

TCP/IP was adopted by ARPANET in 1983 as its standard set of communication rules, defining how data is addressed, broken into packets, and reliably delivered across connected networks. This standardisation allowed many separate networks to interconnect into a single unified system — laying the direct technical foundation of the modern Internet.

Q8.Who invented the World Wide Web, and what was its significance?
5 Marks▼ show answer

The World Wide Web was invented by Tim Berners-Lee around 1989–91. Its significance lies in turning the Internet — until then mainly a research network — into an accessible system of hyperlinked documents that ordinary people could browse using a web browser, sparking the Internet's mass public adoption.

Q9.What is a web link (hyperlink)?
5 Marks▼ show answer

A web link (hyperlink) is a clickable reference embedded in a webpage that takes the user to another page, document, or resource. Hyperlinks are the basic mechanism that lets users "surf" or navigate between connected pages across the World Wide Web.

Q10.Define a browser and list two of its features.
5 Marks▼ show answer

A browser is software used to access, request, and display content from the World Wide Web (e.g. Chrome, Firefox).

Features: an address bar for entering URLs, and bookmarks for saving frequently visited pages (also: tabs, browsing history, extensions).

Q11.Differentiate between an ISP and an OSP.
5 Marks▼ show answer

An Internet Service Provider (ISP) provides users with access/connectivity to the Internet, such as PTCL or a mobile data carrier.

An Online Service Provider (OSP) offers specific curated content or services, sometimes bundled with connectivity — historically exemplified by services like AOL, which combined access with its own proprietary content.

Q12.What is a search engine? Briefly explain how it works.
5 Marks▼ show answer

A search engine (e.g. Google) is a tool that helps users find information on the web. It works in three stages: crawling (scanning web pages), indexing (organising them into a searchable database), and ranking (ordering results by relevance when a query is submitted).

Q13.Define crawling and indexing in the context of search engines.
5 Marks▼ show answer

Crawling is the automated process where bots/spiders continuously scan web pages across the internet to discover content.

Indexing is the process of storing and organising that discovered content into a massive, structured, searchable database so it can be retrieved quickly during a search.

Q14.List any four common services available on the Internet.
5 Marks▼ show answer
  • E-mail — sending and receiving electronic messages.
  • World Wide Web (WWW) — browsing hyperlinked pages.
  • Video conferencing — real-time audio/video calls.
  • Cloud storage — storing and accessing files remotely.
Q15.What is VoIP?
5 Marks▼ show answer

VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol) allows users to make voice calls using an internet data connection instead of a traditional telephone line, converting voice into digital data packets that travel over the internet, then back into audio at the receiving end.

Q16.Differentiate between wired and wireless communication media, giving one example of each.
5 Marks▼ show answer

Wired (cable) media transmit data through a physical conductor that must directly connect the two endpoints. Example: Fiber optic cable.

Wireless media transmit data through the air using electromagnetic signals, without any physical cable connecting the devices. Example: Wi-Fi.
