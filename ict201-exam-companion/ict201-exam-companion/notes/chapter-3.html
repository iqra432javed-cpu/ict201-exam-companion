1. Communication Media Cables & Wireless

Cables

  • Twisted Pair: Two insulated copper wires twisted together; cheap and common in LANs and telephone lines, but limited in distance and speed.
  • Coaxial Cable: A central copper conductor wrapped in shielding; better resistant to interference than twisted pair; historically used for cable TV and early networks.
  • Fiber Optic Cable: Transmits data as pulses of light through glass/plastic strands; the fastest medium, immune to electrical interference, and used for internet backbones and long-distance links.

Wireless Media

  • Radio waves: Travel through air over long distances; basis for many wireless communications.
  • Microwave: Line-of-sight signals used for long-distance point-to-point links between towers.
  • Satellite: Relays signals globally via orbiting satellites — useful where laying cable is impractical.
  • Infrared: Very short-range, line-of-sight signals (e.g. TV remote controls).
  • Bluetooth: Short-range wireless connection between nearby personal devices.
  • Wi-Fi: Wireless local area networking, the wireless equivalent of a LAN.

2. Protocols

A protocol is a set of agreed rules that govern how data is formatted, transmitted, and received between devices, ensuring different systems can communicate reliably.

  • TCP/IP: The foundational protocol suite of the Internet, handling addressing and reliable delivery of data.
  • HTTP/HTTPS: Governs how web pages are requested and delivered between browser and server.
  • FTP: Used for transferring files between computers over a network.
  • SMTP: Governs the sending of email messages.

3. Network Topologies

TopologyLayoutStrengthWeakness
StarAll devices connect to one central hub/switchEasy to manage; one cable fault doesn't crash the networkIf the central hub fails, the whole network goes down
BusAll devices share a single common backbone cableSimple and inexpensive to set upA break in the main cable disrupts the entire network; slows as devices increase
RingEach device connects to exactly two neighbours, forming a closed loopData travels in a predictable, organised pathA single broken connection can disrupt the whole ring (unless a dual ring is used)

5. A Brief History of the Internet & ARPANET

The Internet's roots trace back to ARPANET, a project funded by the U.S. Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) in the late 1960s. Its goal was to let research computers communicate and to build a communication network resilient enough to survive partial failures.

Examiner's FavouriteThe "birthplace of ARPANET" is the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) — the site of its first node, established in 1969. This single fact is a recurring short-question answer.

ARPANET grew through the 1970s and adopted TCP/IP as its standard protocol in 1983, laying the technical foundation of today's Internet. The Internet became truly accessible to the general public after Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web (1989–91) — a system of linked, hyperlinked documents viewable through a browser — turning a research network into the global information system we use today.

6. Web Links & Browsers

A web link (hyperlink) is a clickable reference in a webpage that takes the user to another page or resource — the basic mechanism of navigating, or "browsing," the web.

A browser is the software application used to access, request, and display content from the World Wide Web (e.g. Chrome, Firefox, Edge).

Functions & Features of a Browser

  • Rendering web page content (HTML, CSS, JavaScript) into a readable, interactive page.
  • An address bar for typing or displaying the current URL.
  • Tabs for viewing multiple pages at once.
  • Bookmarks/favourites for saving frequently visited pages.
  • Browsing history and back/forward navigation.
  • Extensions/add-ons for extra functionality, and security indicators (e.g. HTTPS lock icon).

7. Internet Service Provider (ISP) vs Online Service Provider (OSP)

An Internet Service Provider (ISP) is a company that provides users with access/connectivity to the Internet, typically for a subscription fee — e.g. PTCL, Jazz, or any broadband/mobile data provider.

An Online Service Provider (OSP) offers specific online content or services, sometimes bundled together with connectivity, focused on curated content rather than raw access alone (historically exemplified by services like AOL, which combined internet access with its own proprietary content and communities).

8. Search Engines

A search engine (e.g. Google, Bing) helps users locate information on the web. It works in three broad stages:

  1. Crawling — automated programs ("bots" or "spiders") continuously scan web pages.
  2. Indexing — discovered pages are stored and organised in a massive searchable database.

9. Some Common Services Available on the Internet

  • E-mail — sending and receiving electronic messages.
  • World Wide Web (WWW) — browsing hyperlinked pages and resources.
  • FTP — transferring files between computers.
  • Social media — platforms for sharing content and connecting with others.
  • Video conferencing — real-time audio/video communication over the internet.
  • Cloud storage — storing and accessing files remotely over the internet.
  • E-commerce — buying and selling goods/services online.
